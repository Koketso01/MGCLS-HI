About the MGCLS–H I database

The MeerKAT Galaxy Cluster Legacy Survey (MGCLS) is a large programme carried out with the 64-dish MeerKAT telescope in South Africa. Between 2018 and 2019, MeerKAT observed 115 galaxy clusters for 6–10 hours each at L-band (≈900–1670 MHz), producing deep, wide-field radio imaging with typical sensitivities of a few μJy beam−1 and angular resolutions of ≈8 arcsec. The first continuum data release (DR1) provides a rich set of products for studies of radio galaxies, diffuse cluster emission and polarisation.

The MGCLS–H I project builds directly on this legacy by exploiting the neutral hydrogen (H I 21-cm) line information embedded in the same visibilities. By reprocessing the original MGCLS measurement sets in spectral-line mode, the project constructs a homogeneous database of H I detections and data products for galaxies in and around MGCLS clusters. This website hosts the first H I data release (H I–DR1).

Why neutral hydrogen (H I) in galaxy clusters?

Galaxies follow a set of well-known scaling relations that describe their formation and evolution, such as the star-formation main sequence linking stellar mass and star-formation rate. Underlying these relations is a gas-regulation cycle: galaxies acquire gas, store it in their discs, convert part of it into stars, expel material through feedback, and may re-accrete gas over cosmic time.

Neutral atomic hydrogen (H I) is a key component of this cycle. It is a primary reservoir from which molecular gas and stars ultimately form, and it is highly sensitive to environmental processes such as ram-pressure stripping, tidal interactions and “pre-processing” in groups. Measuring where H I is located, how much is present, and how it is removed or redistributed is therefore central to understanding how galaxies grow and quench.

Single-dish surveys (e.g. HIPASS, ALFALFA) have mapped H I over large sky areas but at coarse angular resolution, while interferometric surveys (WHISP, ATLAS3D, BlueDisk, VIVA, THINGS, LVHIS) provide resolved views but typically over smaller areas and with non-uniform depth. As a result, the literature still presents a fragmented picture of how H I content links to stellar mass, environment and star-formation activity, especially in dense environments such as clusters.

MGCLS–H I directly addresses this gap by constructing a homogeneous, cluster-focused, spatially resolved H I dataset across a well-defined sample of galaxy clusters, all observed with the same telescope, frequency setup and reduction pipeline.

From MGCLS to the MGCLS–H I database

The MGCLS–H I project reprocesses the original MGCLS visibilities to extract the 21-cm line signal. The available bandwidth and channelisation allow us to trace H I emission over a primary redshift range of 0 < z < 0.11 (with additional sub-bands at higher redshift), covering galaxies in environments from rich clusters to smaller groups.

The survey strategy is to first process the radio-selected cluster sample with z < 0.1, followed by X-ray selected clusters in the same redshift range. For each cluster, a dedicated H I cube is produced over ≈1280–1430 MHz, covering the relevant velocity range around the cluster systemic redshift.

The goal is to create a legacy H I database that enables robust statistical studies across thousands of galaxies and dozens of clusters, with uniform sensitivity, well-documented completeness and consistent data products. The database is hosted at Rhodes University, and this website provides the first public data release (H I–DR1).

The development of the database and associated workflow has been carried out over a dedicated multi-year effort, in close collaboration with MGCLS team members who initiated the early H I exploration, tuning of pipeline parameters and design of the database schema. This release represents the first step in turning the full MGCLS H I information into a long-lived community resource.

Processing workflow and data preparation

The MGCLS–H I data products are derived from the original MeerKAT visibilities using a uniform spectral-line reduction and staging workflow. At a high level:

  • Raw MeerKAT visibilities are ingested from the science archive.
  • Flagging, calibration and self-calibration are applied using a standard spectral-line reduction pipeline, including radio-frequency interference (RFI) mitigation, cross-calibration and iterative self-calibration.
  • Continuum subtraction and line imaging are performed to isolate the H I line signal, producing primary-beam-corrected data cubes on a regular spatial–spectral grid.
  • 3-D source finding and parameter extraction are run on the cubes to identify H I emission in position–position–velocity space and to measure basic source properties.
  • Completeness testing with mock galaxy injection and recovery experiments is used to quantify the H I mass and linewidth ranges over which the survey is statistically complete, as a function of redshift, linewidth and cube depth.
  • Galaxy-level products (H I cubelets, moment maps, velocity fields, line profiles and masks) are cut out and associated with their parent cluster and catalogued in a consistent schema.
  • Quality assessment and staging are performed before products are ingested into the MGCLS–H I database and exposed through this website.

This end-to-end approach ensures that all clusters in the database are reduced with compatible settings and that the resulting catalogues can be used for homogeneous population studies, completeness-limited analyses and detailed follow-up work.

What is included in MGCLS–H I DR1?

The first MGCLS–H I data release focuses on a staged subset of MGCLS clusters for which the H I cubes and catalogues have passed the current quality checks. For each cluster, the database provides:

  • Cluster-level metadata – basic properties such as redshift, dynamical mass proxies, image depth, beam size and H I column-density sensitivity.
  • Primary-beam-corrected H I cubes – full-field spectral cubes centred on the cluster pointing.
  • Galaxy-level catalogues – H I detections with positions, velocities, integrated fluxes, linewidths and a set of basic morphological descriptors.
  • H I cubelets and moment maps – small cutouts around each detection, including total intensity (moment-0), velocity fields (moment-1) and, where appropriate, velocity-dispersion maps (moment-2).
  • Masks and diagnostic products – source masks, signal-to-noise maps and quality flags that allow users to assess the robustness of each detection.
  • Multi-wavelength preview figures – quick-look overlays that combine H I, X-ray and optical information for many fields.

Example H I clusters in the MGCLS paper

The parent MGCLS paper presents early H I results for four representative galaxy clusters, primarily to demonstrate data quality and science potential: Abell 194, Abell 4038, Abell 3562 and Abell 3365. These clusters lie in the redshift range 0.01 < z < 0.1 and were selected from an X-ray-detected cluster catalogue, spanning a broad range of X-ray luminosity (LX ≈ (0.07–1.3)×1044 erg s−1) and virial mass (Mv ≈ 0.4–2.4×1014 M⊙).

Cluster z (approx.) Environment / mass scale Illustrative H I science
Abell 194 ≈0.018 Low-mass, relatively poor cluster (Mv ~ few ×1013 M⊙) with prominent radio galaxies and extended jets. Resolved H I discs in a group-like environment, probing how gas content and disc structure change in a low-mass cluster and its surroundings.
Abell 4038 ≈0.028 More massive cluster (Mv ~ 2×1014 M⊙) with bright central radio emission and complex ICM structure. H I detections in a dense core environment, useful for studying gas depletion and the deficit of high-mass H I galaxies relative to the field.
Abell 3562 ≈0.049 Cluster in the Shapley supercluster region, at the higher-mass end of the MGCLS example set. Combination of resolved H I structures and disturbed morphologies, tracing environmental processes such as stripping and pre-processing in a complex large-scale structure.
Abell 3365 ≈0.093 Higher-redshift cluster near the upper end of the low-RFI H I window, with more limited sensitivity and stronger RFI constraints. Demonstrates what can be achieved at the high-z end of the MGCLS H I volume, including resolved H I discs in systems with very different radio-continuum properties.

Together, these four clusters highlight the diversity of environments probed by MGCLS–H I and illustrate how the same observing setup can be used to study H I in poor groups, rich clusters and supercluster regions.

Science enabled by the MGCLS–H I database

By combining a well-defined cluster sample with spatially resolved H I measurements, the MGCLS–H I database enables a wide range of environmental and evolutionary studies, including:

  • H I content and deficiency in clusters compared to the field, including trends with cluster-centric distance, local density and projected phase-space location.
  • Gas stripping and morphological transformation, tracing truncated H I discs, one-sided tails and disturbed kinematics associated with ram-pressure stripping, harassment and tidal interactions.
  • Pre-processing in groups and filaments, using galaxies outside the virial radius to study how gas is removed before galaxies reach the cluster core.
  • H I mass function and scaling relations in dense environments, enabling comparisons with single-dish surveys and constraining how H I fractions depend on stellar mass, environment and star-formation activity.
  • Links between H I structure and multi-wavelength tracers, such as X-ray emission from the intracluster medium, optical morphologies and star-formation indicators.

Because the database is homogeneous, well-documented and cluster-centric, it is well suited to both targeted case studies of individual systems and statistical analyses across the ensemble.

Access, documentation and further reading

All released products in MGCLS–H I DR1 are made available via this web interface and are ultimately hosted in an AWS S3 bucket. Users can browse clusters and galaxies, preview figures, and download catalogues, cubelets or full cubes for local analysis.
